# Temporal Stability and Persistence Analysis

## Sustained Cortical State Reorganisation

This directory contains figures and processed CSV outputs examining the temporal stability of cortical activity between the baseline resting-state condition and the modulation condition.

These figures are included as supplementary material for the Influence Warfare Beyond Narratives Cognitive State Manipulation paper. Their purpose is not to demonstrate or validate a particular modulation method. They are intended to illustrate that cognitive state is not only measurable, but may also vary in how long different patterns of neural organisation are maintained over time.

To examine whether the changes identified in the coherence and entropy analyses were transient or sustained, temporal stability metrics were calculated from sliding-window entropy time series across cortical regions and frequency bands. Two complementary measures were evaluated:

* **Autocorrelation decay constant (τ₁/e)** estimates how long regional activity patterns persist before returning toward baseline.
* **Mean dwell time above baseline** measures how long a region remains within an elevated dynamical state.

The analyses demonstrated selective increases in temporal persistence rather than uniform stabilisation across the cortex. Several regional-frequency combinations exhibited longer autocorrelation decay constants and extended dwell times, indicating that some patterns of neural organisation remained stable for longer than others.

These findings extend the coherence and entropy analyses by showing that the observed changes in cortical organisation were maintained over meaningful periods rather than occurring as isolated or transient events.

The significance of these figures is conceptual rather than clinical. They illustrate that cognitive state is not defined solely by instantaneous patterns of neural activity, but also by the stability with which those patterns are maintained over time.

If cognitive state influences how information is processed, then the persistence of that state may influence how long particular patterns of attention, perception, and information processing remain active before the brain transitions toward a different operating condition.
